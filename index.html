Global Warming and Its Impact on Egypt

If we suppose that planet Earth is four and a half billion years old, then the phenomena of global warming is just approximately two hundred years old. Simply expressed, it is the rise in temperature in the lowest layer of the Earth's atmosphere, due to the production of vast volumes of carbon dioxide, methane, and other gases.

The connection between rising temperatures and greenhouse gas emissions lies in the mechanism of global warming itself. It is well recognized that the sun is the planet Earth's main source of heat. Once sunlight strikes the Earth's surface, a portion of the heat it transmits is absorbed, while the remainder is reflected and returned to space. However, greenhouse gases in the atmosphere absorb this heat, preventing it from escaping and contributing to an increase in temperature.

Impacts on Egypt

Egypt is already located in a hot region, and global warming has significant economic and environmental implications. The most notable impacts include:

1. Rising Sea Levels

Studies suggest that by the end of the century, sea levels in Alexandria and the Nile Delta might rise by 50 to 100 centimeters. Approximately 7 million people would be impacted, and around 30% of food production in these areas would be at risk if 12% to 15% of the Delta's lowlands were submerged.

2. Water Scarcity and Drought

97% of Egypt's water demands are met by the Nile River. Global warming may decrease the flow of the Nile by as much as 15% by 2050, severely affecting Upper Egypt's agriculture and irrigation.

3. Effects on Agriculture

Over the next several decades, Egypt's temperature is predicted to increase by 2 to 3 degrees Celsius. This rise could significantly affect crop output, with wheat yields potentially declining by up to 11% and maize by 19%.

4. Increased Heat in Major Cities

Cairo may experience a considerable increase in the number of intense heat wave days, jumping from 8 days at the beginning of the century to approximately 50 days by 2050.

5. Health Impacts

Rising temperatures will lead to increased heat-related disorders, particularly in cities like Cairo, Giza, and Aswan, along with declining air quality leading to more respiratory ailments.

Monitoring Greenhouse Gases

Methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) concentrations are measured using various sophisticated methods:

  • Infrared Absorption Analysis: This technique relies on the gases' ability to absorb infrared light at specific wavelengths.
  • Laser Analysis: Methods like Tunable Diode Laser Absorption Spectroscopy measure gas concentrations through laser beams.
  • Laboratory Examination: Air samples are analyzed in labs using equipment like mass spectrometers and gas chromatography.
  • Satellite Sensing: Satellites like TROPOMI and GOSAT gather data on global gas quantities through light absorption.
  • Ground Monitoring Stations: Fixed sensors assess gas concentrations continuously, such as the Mauna Loa Observatory.
  • Drones: Drones equipped with sensors monitor gas concentrations in hard-to-reach areas.
